We are one

Taiwan's journey towards marriage equality

"For Asia’s Gays, Taiwan Stands Out as Beacon"

    Taiwan, recongnized as the most gay-friendly country in Asia, has come a long way to finally see the light of becoming the first in the region to legalize same-sex marriage. In the process of fighting for marriage equality, gay rights movements in Taiwan were highly associated with movements aiming for women's rights and basic human rights. I would like to briefly comb the history of major events and movements which distinctly contributed to the promise of marriage equality in Taiwan.

Brief History

The Awakening

    In 1982, a group of women published the first Awakening Magazine aiming for promote women's rights and self-awareness. Later on in 1987, the group members established Awakening Foundation and the foundation has been actively advocating women's rights ever since and playing a key role in fighting for gender equality in Taiwan.

The Pioneer

    Chi Chia-wei is the first person to publicly come out as gay in Taiwan. In March 1986, he held a press conference and appeared on national television to announce his sexuality and the launch of a campaign to prevent the spread of AIDS. He then became the most important gay rights activist in Taiwan and a figure in all the gay rights movements during three decades.

Warm Life

    In 1996, the director of the DPP's Women's Affairs Department, former secrectary-general of Awakening Foundation, an active feminist Taiwanese politician, Peng Wan-ru was discovered raped and murdered in an abandoned warehouse. The incident led to an outcry against the lack of women's protection in Taiwan. Peng's proposed quota for one fourth of the seats of the elected seats to be reserved for women was quickly passed afterwards. An amendment of Civil Code proposed by Warm Life, an organization advocating women's protection, went through three readings and was passed, changing women's inferior place on legal matters. Peng's death also led to mass demonstrations in May 1997 and the following movements of women's rights.

Hotline

In 1998, Taiwan Tongzhi Hotline Association was established and became one of the major organizations which advocate LGBT rights. Their work includes providing LGBT people with peer counseling, support networks, a community resource center. They also have been supoorting the promotion of gender equality education in Taiwan.

The Rose Boy

    On one morning of April in 2000, Yeh Yung-chih, a 15 year-old boy, asked the teacher to go to the bathroom during the class. Moments later, his cold body was found on the bathroom in a pool of blood. In his school years, Yeh had been constantly bullied by his schoolmates because of his effeminate behavior and gender expression. Yeh's death led to the re-awareness of gender education in school. The Ministry of Education transformed its Gender Equality Education Committee(literally translated as "the committee of equality education of two sexes" in Chinese) into the broader Gender Equity Education Committee to promote diverse gender education beyond the two gender stereotypes. In 2004, the Gender Equity Education Act was enacted by the government.

Pride

    In 2003, the first Taiwan Pride parade took place in Taipei. It was also the first pride parade in Asia. The annual event soon became one of the largest gay pride event in East Asia. The most recent one was held on 28th October, 2017, attracting more than 11 million people around the world.

    In 2006, member of Legislative Yuan, Hsiao Bi-khim proposed bill draft to the Civil Code but it was turned down in the Procedure Committee.

    In 2013, the Taiwan Alliance to Promote Civil Partnership Rights released the first draft revisions to the Civil Code(regarding same-sex marriage, civil partnerships and multiple-person families) and proposed it to the Legislative Yuan.

    In 2014, member of Legislative Yuan, Yu Mei-nu again proposed bill draft to the Civil Code regarding same-sex marriage. It went through the first reading and made it to the Judiciary and Organic Laws Committee.

    In 2016, part of the members of Legislative Yuan, including of parties such as DPP, KMT and NPP, once again proposed bill draft to the Civil Code in order to legalize same-sex marriage. It went through the first reading and the Judiciary and Organic Laws Committee, entering the multi-party caucus negotiation.

The Ruling

    In 2013, Chi Chia-wei once again applied for a marriage license as a same-sex couple. The application was denied and he appealed upward, bringing the constitutionality question up to the Supreme Administrative Court.

    On May 24, 2017, the Constitutional Court ruled in Interpretation No. 748 that the Civil Code's restriction of marriage to only between a man and a woman is unconstitutional and the legislature should either amend the Civil Code or enact laws addressing same-sex couples within two years. If not, by two years, same-sex couples will automatically be allowed to have their marriage registration under the current Civil Code.
